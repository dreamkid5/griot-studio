At two o'clock in the morning, on the twentieth of June, 1942, a night watchman walked into a darkened room inside the French embassy in Washington, D.C.

He was carrying a flashlight. He was doing his rounds. And what he found in that room should have ended the Second World War differently.

Because in the corner, beside a steel safe that held the naval codes of an entire nation, stood a woman. She was almost completely naked. Her clothes were scattered across the carpet. A man crouched behind her, half dressed, frozen in place.

And in the shadows behind them both, invisible, holding his breath, was a professional safecracker on loan from the United States government, with his tools spread out on the floor.

The watchman stared. The woman stared back. She did not scream. She did not run. She did not reach for a weapon.

She smiled.

And then she said, in perfect French, that she was terribly sorry, and that surely a man of the world would understand what a woman and a married man were doing in an empty embassy at two in the morning.

The watchman apologized. He backed out of the room. He closed the door behind him.

He had just walked past the single most damaging espionage operation of the American war effort, and he never knew it.

Her name was Amy Elizabeth Thorpe. The world would come to know her as Cynthia. And by the time she was finished, she had used her body, her charm, and her absolutely lethal intelligence to strip the secrets out of an Italian admiral, a Polish government, and the entire French naval cipher system.

Thousands of men would live because of what she stole. Thousands more would die.

This is her story. And I promise you, it gets stranger than the room you just walked into.
She was born in Minneapolis in November of 1910, the daughter of a United States Marine Corps officer and a woman who was ferociously, exhaustingly ambitious on her behalf.

From the beginning, there was something about Amy Elizabeth Thorpe that unsettled people. She was beautiful, yes. Green eyes, golden hair, a face that photographers loved. But beauty was common in Washington society, and beauty alone does not build a spy.

What she had was something rarer. She had no fear.

Not courage, exactly. Courage implies you feel the fear and push through it. Amy Elizabeth Thorpe seems to have simply been born without the wiring. Danger did not raise her pulse. It bored her when it was absent.

By her teenage years she was already a fixture at Washington parties, moving among senators and generals and diplomats with the ease of a woman thirty years older. She was writing poetry. She was reading obsessively. And she was, by every account including her own, profoundly, painfully restless.

And then, at nineteen, she did what young women of her class did in 1930. She married.

His name was Arthur Pack. He was a British diplomat, a commercial attaché, and he was almost twice her age. He was cautious. He was proper. He was, in the most complete sense of the word, dull.

She was pregnant before the wedding, which in 1930 was a scandal that had to be surgically managed. The child was born in England and quietly placed with a foster family. She would barely see him again.

Sit with that for a second, because it tells you everything about who she was. Most people carry a wound like that and it makes them softer. It made her harder. It taught her, at twenty years old, that her own life could be edited. That the past could be closed like a door.

The marriage carried her to Chile. Then to Spain. And in Spain, in 1936, the world she had been waiting for finally arrived.

The Spanish Civil War broke out around her, and Amy Elizabeth Thorpe discovered something about herself that would define the rest of her existence.

While her husband worried about protocol, she was smuggling people out of the country. Using her diplomatic status, her charm, her nerve, and a Red Cross armband, she moved people across lines that should have killed her. She hid fugitives. She talked her way through checkpoints run by men with rifles and no patience.

And she loved it.

Not the politics. She barely had any at that point. She loved the feeling. The moment when a soldier looks at your papers, and looks at your face, and everything in the world hangs on whether you can hold his eyes for three more seconds without blinking.

Somewhere in that country, a bored diplomat's wife discovered that she was, in fact, a natural predator.

The British Secret Intelligence Service was watching. They took notes. They filed her name away.

They would come back for her.
In 1937, Arthur Pack was posted to Warsaw. And Poland, in 1937, was the most dangerous room in Europe.

Hitler was to the west. Stalin was to the east. And in the middle sat a proud, doomed nation whose cryptographers were quietly performing one of the greatest intellectual feats of the twentieth century.

Polish mathematicians were breaking the German Enigma machine.

Understand what that means. Enigma was the encryption device the German military believed to be unbreakable. It scrambled their orders, their fleet positions, their attack plans, into gibberish that the Germans were certain no enemy could ever unravel. It was, to them, a mathematical wall around the Reich.

And a small team of Polish codebreakers had found the crack in it.

Now. Amy Elizabeth Thorpe, twenty six years old, arrived in Warsaw as a diplomat's wife. She threw parties. She learned the language. She was invited everywhere, because she was charming and beautiful and she made powerful men feel like the most interesting person in the room, which is a skill that has toppled more governments than gunpowder.

And she began, very deliberately, to take lovers.

Not for pleasure. Or not only for pleasure. She selected them.

One was a senior official in the Polish foreign ministry. Another was closer still, a man who worked at the right hand of the Polish foreign minister himself, in the very office where the nation's secrets crossed a desk every morning.

She listened. She asked the kind of light, wide eyed questions that no man suspects, the questions that sound like admiration and function like a scalpel. And in the warm, unguarded quiet after, when men talk because talking feels like intimacy, she gathered what she needed.

She learned about German intentions toward Czechoslovakia before the crisis broke. She learned about Polish assessments of Hitler's timetable. And she brushed against the greatest secret in Europe, the Polish work on Enigma, at the exact moment when British intelligence was desperate to understand whether such a thing was even possible.

The British quietly credited her with material of real value. Some of her later admirers claimed she practically handed Britain the Enigma solution. That is an exaggeration, and I want to be straight with you about it, because the truth is remarkable enough without inflation. The Poles handed their Enigma breakthrough to Britain and France directly, in a forest outside Warsaw, in July of 1939.

But here is what matters. In Warsaw, Amy Elizabeth Thorpe proved a thesis. She proved that a woman who is patient, fearless, and completely unbothered by conventional shame could walk into the most guarded rooms in Europe and walk out with what was inside them.

The Secret Intelligence Service made it official. She was recruited. She was given a code name.

Cynthia.

And then the war came, and Cynthia was sent to the one place where she could do the most damage.

She was sent home. To America. You have to understand the desperation of Britain in 1941 to understand what happens next.

Britain was alone. France had fallen. The Soviet Union was still, at that point, on friendly terms with Hitler. America was neutral, isolationist, and full of powerful men who thought the entire thing was a European problem best solved by staying home.

So the British ran an intelligence operation on American soil. It was called British Security Coordination, it was headquartered in Rockefeller Center in New York, and its unofficial mission was to drag the United States into the war by any means available.

Cynthia was handed a target in Washington.

His name was Admiral Alberto Lais. He was the Italian naval attaché. He was a man in his sixties, distinguished, decorated, and lonely in a foreign capital. Italy was at war with Britain. The Italian fleet was the strongest naval force in the Mediterranean, and the British could not read its signals.

Cynthia went to work.

She met him. She flattered him. She was warm, attentive, and she let him believe that he had found something almost miraculous at the end of a long career, a young, beautiful woman who genuinely, helplessly adored him.

He called her his little girl.

And she let him.

Now here is where I want you to notice something, because this is what separates Cynthia from every cheap movie version of the seductress spy. She did not simply crawl into a bed and wait for secrets to fall out. She built an entire emotional architecture around this man. She learned his fears. She learned that he was unhappy with the war. She learned that he thought Mussolini was leading Italy into catastrophe.

And then, very gently, she opened a door for him.

She told him she was working for people who wanted the war to end. She asked him to help her. She made betraying his own navy feel less like treason and more like an act of love, and rescue, and moral courage.

He gave her access to the Italian naval ciphers.

The exact chain of custody has been argued about for eighty years. Cynthia claimed she persuaded him to help arrange it. Lais, after the war, furiously denied everything until his death. His family denies it still.

But this is what history records. In the spring of 1941, the British suddenly began reading Italian naval traffic with terrifying precision.

And on the night of the twenty eighth of March, 1941, off Cape Matapan on the southern coast of Greece, the Royal Navy ambushed the Italian fleet in the dark.

The Italians had no idea the British were there. They had no idea the British knew exactly where they were.

Three heavy cruisers and two destroyers were destroyed. Over two thousand Italian sailors died in the water that night. The Italian navy was so badly mauled that it never again seriously contested the Mediterranean.

A beautiful woman had whispered to a lonely old man in Washington, and a fleet died in the Mediterranean.

Cynthia never expressed a moment of regret about it. Not one. When people later asked her whether she felt any shame about the way she had done her work, her answer was essentially this: the men she seduced were enemies, the information saved lives, and she would do all of it again tomorrow.

She was, by any measure you choose, a genuinely frightening human being.

And her greatest operation had not even begun. By late 1941, Cynthia had a new target, and it was the biggest one yet.

The Vichy French embassy in Washington.

After France fell, a collaborationist French government was installed at Vichy, and it still controlled the French colonies in North Africa. It still had a navy. It still had embassies, including a large, active one in Washington, D.C.

And inside that embassy, in a safe, sat the Vichy French naval ciphers.

Here is why they mattered. The Allies were planning Operation Torch, the invasion of French North Africa. It would be the first great Allied offensive of the war, over a hundred thousand men landing on hostile beaches in Morocco and Algeria.

And nobody knew whether the French forces there would fight or surrender. Nobody knew how the Vichy fleet would respond. If the French navy resisted with everything it had, Torch could turn into a bloodbath before a single Allied soldier reached the sand.

Those ciphers would let the Allies read Vichy's mind.

Cynthia's route to the safe was a man named Charles Brousse. He was the embassy press attaché. He was married. He was a decorated pilot from the first war. He was also, at that moment, a man in his fifties with an expensive wife, financial anxiety, and a slowly curdling contempt for the collaborationist government he served.

She approached him as a journalist. Then as a friend. Then as something considerably more.

And here is the part of the story that always undoes people, because this is where the ice cracks.

Cynthia fell in love with him.

Not the performance. The real thing. She, the woman who had used a dozen men like tools and discarded them without a backward glance, fell in love with her own target. And astonishingly, so did he.

He agreed to help her, and the debate about his motives has never been settled. Money, certainly, some of it British money. Patriotism, probably. He believed Vichy had betrayed France. But love was in there too, and Brousse would spend the rest of his life insisting it was the largest part.

Together they built the cover story that would let them into the building.

Brousse told the embassy night guard that he was conducting an affair with a young American woman. He asked, man to man, embarrassed, if he might occasionally use one of the quiet rooms in the embassy late at night, because his wife was watching him and there was nowhere else to go.

The guard, being French, and being human, thought this was hilarious and touching in equal measure.

For weeks they came at night. They talked. They embraced. They let the guard see just enough. They brought him champagne. They made themselves boring. They made themselves expected.

They were not building a romance. They were building a habit. Because the greatest weapon in espionage is not a gun or a code. It is the moment when a guard sees you and stops seeing you at all. 
The night of the twentieth of June, 1942.

Cynthia had a professional with her. American intelligence, the office that would become the OSS, had produced a safecracker. He was a convicted criminal who had been quietly released from prison to serve his country. In the accounts he is remembered only by a nickname, the Georgia Cracker.

The plan was simple, which is another way of saying it had a hundred ways to fail.

Brousse and Cynthia arrive as usual. The guard sees them, laughs, leaves them alone. They drug the guard's champagne so he sleeps. The safecracker slips in through a window. He opens the safe. The ciphers are photographed. Everything goes back exactly where it was. Nobody ever knows.

Almost every part of it went wrong.

The guard's dog, which nobody had accounted for, was in the building.

The safecracker took far longer than promised. The lock resisted him. Minutes bled away. Then more minutes. The window of darkness they had was closing, and the safe was still shut.

And then the footsteps came down the corridor.

The watchman was doing an unscheduled round. He was heading directly for the room where a professional criminal was crouched over an open toolkit beside the naval secrets of France.

And in the seconds she had, Cynthia made a decision that is still taught, quietly, in intelligence circles as a masterclass in what nerve actually looks like.

She tore off her clothes.

She stripped down to almost nothing and she arranged herself and Brousse into the unmistakable shape of two lovers interrupted, and she waited, in the dark, for a man with a flashlight to open the door.

He opened it. The beam found her.

And she looked at him, calm, unhurried, mortified in exactly the way a woman caught in an affair would be mortified, and she apologized for the inconvenience.

The watchman, who had spent weeks being told a story about a married man and his mistress, saw the story he had been told. That is the entire secret of espionage in one sentence. People do not see what is in front of them. They see what they have been prepared to see.

He apologized to her. He withdrew. The door closed.

And in the darkness on the far side of that room, pressed flat and utterly silent, the safecracker exhaled.

They got the safe open. They photographed the ciphers. The material went out of that embassy and into British and American hands, and within months, the Allies were reading Vichy naval communications.

On the eighth of November, 1942, Operation Torch went in. Over a hundred thousand men landed in North Africa. Allied commanders knew, in advance, the state of French forces, the disposition of the fleet, and where resistance would be soft.

French resistance collapsed within days. The casualties, for an amphibious invasion of that size, were remarkably light.

It was the turning of the war in the west. And it began, in part, with a naked woman smiling at a night watchman.

So what happens to a woman like that when the war ends?

Arthur Pack, the dull husband, took his own life in 1945.

Cynthia married Charles Brousse. They moved to France, to a crumbling stone castle in the Pyrenees called Castelnou, and there she lived out the rest of her life with the one target she had never been able to discard.

She wrote. She talked to a biographer. She defended everything she had ever done with a bluntness that shocked people. She had no apology in her. She was asked, more than once, whether she was ashamed of using sex as a weapon. Her answer was always some version of the same thing. It was a weapon. She used it. Men used bombs.

The intelligence services who had used her did not exactly celebrate her. They were embarrassed by her. The men who ran the war were happy to spend her and considerably less happy to explain her afterward. A woman who kills with a battleship gets a medal. A woman who kills with a bedroom gets a whisper.

She died of cancer in 1963. She was fifty three years old. She was buried in France.

And here, at the end, is the thing I cannot stop turning over.

She gave up her child. She burned through her marriages. She used every intimate thing a human being has to give, and she used it as a tool, and she did it so effectively that she may have shortened the deadliest war in history.

Was she a hero? She helped drown two thousand men at Cape Matapan and she helped save far more than that in North Africa.

Was she a monster? She never once claimed to be anything else, and she never once asked to be forgiven.

Maybe the truest thing that can be said about Amy Elizabeth Thorpe is that the world went to war, and she finally, at long last, was not bored.
