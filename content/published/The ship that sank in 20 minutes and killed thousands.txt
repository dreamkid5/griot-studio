On the morning of May 1st, 1915, a woman in New York opened her newspaper to book a voyage.

She found the Cunard advertisement exactly where she expected it. The Lusitania. The fastest ocean liner in the world. Departing Pier 54 at noon, bound for Liverpool.

And printed directly beside it, in the very same column, was a second notice. A warning. Placed by the Imperial German Embassy in Washington.

It reminded travelers that a state of war existed between Germany and Great Britain. That the war zone included the waters around the British Isles. That vessels flying the British flag were liable to destruction in those waters. And that anyone who sailed into that zone did so at their own risk.

Read that again. A foreign government bought newspaper space in the United States to tell American citizens, by name and by ship, that they might die.

And here is the part that should make the hair stand up on your arms.

They boarded anyway. Nearly two thousand of them. They laughed about it on the pier. They joked with reporters. A millionaire waved off the warning as a stunt. A famous theatre producer said he was not afraid.

Six days later, one thousand one hundred and ninety eight of those people were dead.

Not in a night. Not in an hour. In eighteen minutes.

This is the story of the warning nobody believed, the torpedo that struck like a hammer on a bell, the second explosion that nobody can explain to this day, and a British Admiralty that has been accused, for more than a century, of letting it happen.

My name is your host, and this is the last voyage of the Lusitania.
To understand why nobody believed the warning, you have to understand what this ship was.

The Lusitania was not merely a boat. She was a national argument made of steel. Seven hundred and eighty seven feet of her. Four towering funnels. Turbine engines that could push more than thirty thousand tons of ship through the Atlantic at a speed that no submarine on earth could match.

She was, at her launch, the largest moving object ever built by human hands. She took the Blue Riband, the unofficial crown for the fastest crossing of the Atlantic. She was Britain's answer to German shipbuilding, and Britain built her with money quietly loaned by the Royal Navy, on the condition that she could be converted into an armed cruiser if war ever came.

Remember that detail. It matters later.

Inside, she was a floating palace. Mahogany panels. A first class dining saloon rising two decks high beneath a domed ceiling of stained glass. Electric lifts. Marble. Silk. A world of oysters and orchestras.

And the passengers believed something about her that turned out to be the deadliest belief of the entire war.

They believed she was too fast to be caught.

Submarines in 1915 crawled. On the surface they made perhaps fifteen knots. Underwater, less than half of that. The Lusitania at full stride could make twenty five. The mathematics seemed unanswerable. A submarine could only kill what it could reach, and no submarine could reach her.

There was only one flaw in that beautiful piece of arithmetic.

It assumed the Lusitania would be running at full speed.

She would not be.
Her captain was William Thomas Turner. Sixty years old. A sailor since he was a boy, a man who had once dived into a harbor to save a drowning child, a man who disliked passengers and said so, who called the wealthy first class travelers a lot of bloody monkeys and preferred the honest company of the sea.

Turner was not reckless. He was, by every account, a superb seaman. But he was sailing under a set of contradictions that no seaman could reconcile.

Contradiction one. Cunard was losing money on the wartime Atlantic. To save coal, the company shut down one of the ship's four boiler rooms. That single accounting decision cut her top speed from twenty five knots to about twenty one. The margin of safety, the very thing that made her uncatchable, was quietly reduced by a bookkeeper.

Contradiction two. The Admiralty had issued instructions to merchant captains in the war zone. Steer a zigzag course. Stay away from headlands. Keep to the middle of the channel. Maintain full speed. But those instructions were vague, they were new, and they conflicted with the oldest instinct in navigation, which is to know exactly where you are before you make landfall.

And contradiction three. The most damning one. In a locked room in London, at the Admiralty building, a team of code breakers in a section known only as Room Forty had been reading German naval signals for months.

They knew a submarine had left Germany. They knew roughly where it was operating. They knew that in the days before the Lusitania approached Ireland, ships were being sunk in the exact waters she was steaming toward.

Three ships had already gone down in that patch of sea. Right in her path.

Captain Turner was not told.

He received general warnings. Submarines active off the south coast of Ireland. Avoid headlands. Nothing specific. Nothing that said, in plain English, there is a killer waiting at the mouth of your channel and we know it.

Why not?

That question has never been answered to everyone's satisfaction. And we will come back to it, because it is the beating heart of this mystery.
His name was Walther Schwieger. He was thirty years old. Blond, courteous, well liked by his crew, a man who kept a dachshund aboard his submarine.

He commanded a vessel designated U 20.

Understand what a submarine was in 1915. Not a sleek machine. A cramped iron tube that stank of diesel and unwashed men and rotting food. Air so foul that matches would not stay lit. Condensation dripping constantly from the ceiling. Crews lived in a permanent state of damp misery, and they went to sea knowing that the ocean floor was the likeliest grave they would get.

Schwieger's patrol had gone badly. Fog. Mechanical trouble. He was down to his last torpedoes and his fuel was running low. He had already decided to turn for home.

And then, on the afternoon of May 7th, the fog lifted.

The Lusitania had spent the morning in that same fog, creeping along at reduced speed, sounding her foghorn, which is precisely what a captain must do to avoid a collision and precisely what a captain must never do if he is trying to hide.

When the mist cleared, Turner needed a fix on his position. Ahead of him lay the Old Head of Kinsale, a great green fist of rock punching out into the Atlantic, one of the most recognizable landmarks in Ireland. To confirm his location he took a bearing on it. That maneuver required him to hold a steady course for roughly forty minutes.

A straight line. No zigzag. Reduced speed. Close to a headland.

Every single instruction he had been given, broken at once, for the most defensible reason a navigator can have.

Through the periscope of U 20, at a distance of several miles, Walther Schwieger watched four funnels rise out of the haze.

He wrote in his war diary that the ship was so enormous, and so unmistakable, that he could hardly believe what he was seeing.

He gave the order to close.

And then, in one of the strangest coincidences in maritime history, the Lusitania turned.

She turned toward him. Turner had begun a slow starboard swing to line up his approach to Queenstown. He had, without the faintest idea he was doing it, delivered his ship into a perfect firing position.

Schwieger waited. The range closed to seven hundred meters.

At ten minutes past two in the afternoon, he fired one torpedo.
A lookout in the bow saw it first. A white streak on the water, thin as a chalk line, running fast and true toward the starboard side.

He shouted. There was no time to do anything else.

The torpedo struck below the bridge, just behind the front of the ship. Survivors described the sound in strikingly similar terms. Not a bang. A deep, dull thud, like a heavy door slamming somewhere far below, followed by a shudder that ran through the whole hull.

And then, seconds later, came the thing that no one has ever fully explained.

A second explosion.

It was far larger than the first. It blew a column of water and coal and debris high above the bridge. It tore the guts out of the forward section of the ship. Passengers on deck were thrown from their feet. In the boiler rooms, high pressure steam lines ruptured and men were scalded where they stood.

Schwieger, watching through his periscope, was startled by it. He recorded in his log that a second detonation followed the first, one he had not caused and could not account for.

He fired one torpedo. Everyone agrees on that. He had another aboard and did not use it. He did not need to.

Whatever that second explosion was, it killed the Lusitania.

The ship immediately took a savage list to starboard. Within minutes she was leaning at fifteen degrees. Then twenty. And she was still moving forward at nearly eighteen knots, because the explosion had wrecked the steam lines and the bridge could no longer send an order to the engine room to stop.

This is the detail that turned a disaster into a massacre.

She had forty eight lifeboats. On paper, more than enough for everyone aboard. In reality, the list made them nearly useless.

On the starboard side, the high side of the list, the boats swung outward, away from the hull, leaving a yawning gap of open air and water between the deck and the boat. Passengers had to jump across it. Some made it. Some did not.

On the port side, the boats swung inward and dragged along the ship's flank, catching on the rivets, on the deck plates, on everything that stood proud of the hull. Crews hacked at ropes to free them. One boat broke loose and swung like a wrecking ball across a crowded deck. Another spilled its occupants sixty feet into the sea. Another was crushed against the hull as the ship rolled onward.

And all the while the Lusitania was still sailing. Still driving forward. Boats that touched the water were dragged under or swamped or smashed against the moving steel wall of the ship.

Of forty eight lifeboats, six got away cleanly.

Six.

Below decks it was worse than anything on the surface. The electrical system failed almost at once. The great electric lifts, which had been carrying passengers up from the lower decks when the torpedo hit, stopped between floors. The men and women inside them could not be reached. They were still calling out when the water arrived.

On the upper decks, there was courage. There was an American millionaire named Alfred Vanderbilt, one of the richest men alive, who was seen giving away his lifebelt and helping to tie lifebelts onto children. He could not swim. He did not survive.

There was a theatre producer named Charles Frohman, leaning on a rail, calm to the point of serenity, who told those around him that death was nothing to fear, that it was simply the most beautiful adventure in life. He did not survive either.

And there were the ordinary ones. Stokers climbing up out of flooding boiler rooms with steam burns across their backs. A stewardess handing children through a doorway. A father tying his daughter to a deck chair because it was the only thing that would float.

The bow struck the seabed while the stern was still high in the air. The ship was longer than the water was deep. For a few seconds she stood there, impaled on the bottom of the Atlantic, her four funnels still smoking, her propellers turning uselessly in the sky.

Then she slid under.

From the first sight of that white streak to the moment the sea closed over her stern, eighteen minutes had passed.

The Titanic took two hours and forty minutes to sink, and lost roughly one third fewer lives.
The Lusitania went down eleven miles off the Irish coast. Close enough that survivors could see the green fields of Kinsale.

That closeness was a cruelty of its own. The water was around fifty two degrees Fahrenheit. Cold enough to kill by exhaustion and hypothermia in under an hour for most people. Hundreds of passengers who survived the sinking itself did not survive the wait.

Some had put their lifebelts on incorrectly, and the belts turned them face down in the water and held them there. Rescuers pulling bodies from the sea found people floating upside down, kept afloat by the very device meant to save them.

Fishing boats from Kinsale and Queenstown put out at once. They rowed and sailed into a field of wreckage and bodies and screaming. They pulled out everyone they could reach. Those Irish fishermen saved hundreds of lives that afternoon, and it has always seemed to me that history has not thanked them nearly enough.

Of one thousand nine hundred and fifty nine people aboard, seven hundred and sixty one lived.

One hundred and twenty eight of the dead were American citizens.

Ninety four of the dead were children. Thirty one of them were babies.

Queenstown became a morgue. Bodies were laid out in rows on the quayside. Hotels became mortuaries. Coffins were built as fast as carpenters could work, and there were not enough. Families arrived by train from England for days afterward, walking those rows, looking for faces.
And now we come to the part of this story that has never been closed.

Because there are questions, and every one of them has teeth.

Question one. What was the second explosion?

The official inquiry suggested a second torpedo, which we now know is false. Schwieger fired one. Other explanations followed. A boiler explosion, though the boilers were later found intact. Coal dust in the nearly empty bunkers, ignited by the torpedo blast. A rupture of high pressure steam mains.

Or, the explanation that will not die. The cargo.

Because the Lusitania was carrying munitions. This is not conspiracy theory. This is on her own manifest. More than four thousand cases of rifle cartridges. Empty shell casings. Fuses. The British government admitted the cartridges at the time and argued, correctly, that small arms ammunition of that type is remarkably difficult to detonate in bulk.

But then why, for decades afterward, did the Royal Navy conduct depth charge exercises directly over the wreck site? Why has diving on the wreck been so persistently discouraged and restricted? Divers who have gone down to her have reported finding ammunition in the hull. What else is down there in the sediment of that broken bow, which is precisely the section where the second explosion occurred?

Question two, and this is the one that keeps historians awake.

Why was she not protected?

Room Forty knew a submarine was hunting in her path. The Admiralty had a cruiser, the Juno, available at Queenstown that could have escorted her in. The Juno was recalled to harbor and no replacement was sent. The Lusitania was left to steam through a known killing ground, at reduced speed, on a straight course, without an escort and without being given the specific intelligence that would have let her captain understand the danger he was in.

There are those who have argued, from that day to this, that certain men in London understood something coldly and clearly. That Britain was bleeding to death in the trenches. That the only thing that could win the war was American entry. And that nothing on earth would drag a reluctant America into that war faster than the sight of American women and children drowning in the Atlantic.

Was the Lusitania offered as bait?

Most serious historians say no. They say the truth is duller and, in its way, more damning. That the Admiralty was overwhelmed, badly organized, obsessed with protecting the secret that it could read German codes, and simply too complacent to imagine that a passenger liner would be torpedoed without warning.

Not a conspiracy. A catastrophic failure of care.

But that answer has never quite satisfied anybody. And the way the British government behaved afterward did not help.

Because at the official inquiry, the man they went after was Captain Turner.

Turner had survived. He had gone down with his ship, been sucked under, blown back to the surface, and clung to a floating oar for three hours. And now he sat in a courtroom while the Admiralty briefed against him, blamed him, and worked to hang the whole catastrophe around the neck of a man who had never been told what they knew.

The judge, Lord Mersey, cleared Turner. He put the blame squarely on Germany.

And then Lord Mersey did something extraordinary. He resigned from the case. He waived his fee. And privately he described the whole Lusitania affair, in words that have echoed for a hundred years, as a damned dirty business.

He never explained what he meant.
Walther Schwieger did not live to see the end of the war. He was killed in 1917 when his submarine struck a mine. He was thirty two.

Captain Turner went back to sea and was torpedoed a second time. He survived that too. He lived out his years in Liverpool, quiet and bitter, feeding pigeons, refusing to discuss the ship.

Germany, stunned by the international fury, pulled back from unrestricted submarine warfare for nearly two years. When they resumed it in 1917, gambling that they could starve Britain before America could mobilize, they lost that gamble. The United States declared war in April of that year, and the recruiting posters carried a single word above the image of a drowning woman and her child.

Lusitania.

She lies today about three hundred feet down, eleven miles off the Old Head of Kinsale. She is not upright and dignified like the Titanic. She lies collapsed on her starboard side, crushed by the pressure and the years and, some say, by the depth charges. She is falling in on herself, and within a few decades there will be very little of her left to find.

And so what remains is a warning, printed in a newspaper column on the first of May, 1915, that nearly two thousand people read and dismissed.

They looked at that notice, and they looked at the greatest ship on the ocean, and they decided that nothing so beautiful and so fast and so modern could possibly be sunk by a rusting iron tube full of desperate men.

They were wrong by eighteen minutes.

If this story got its hooks in you, subscribe. Next time, we are going down with a ship that nobody was ever supposed to know about, one that took nine thousand people to the bottom of the Baltic, and whose name the world has almost entirely forgotten.
