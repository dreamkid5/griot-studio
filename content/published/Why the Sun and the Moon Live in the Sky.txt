In the beginning, when the world was young, the Sun and the Moon did not live in the sky at all. They lived on the earth, in a fine wide compound, as husband and wife, and they were the warmest of neighbours to all who passed their door.

Their dearest friend was the Water. Yet though the Sun visited the Water in his great blue home again and again, the Water never once came to visit them. This puzzled the Sun. Friend, he said at last, why do you never sit at my table? Have I not walked all this way to sit at yours?

The Water bowed his cool head. My friend, it is not for want of love, he said. It is that I am very many. If I came to your compound with all my people, we would fill it and spill it and drive you from your own home. Your house is simply too small for me.

Then I shall build a house so grand, said the proud and generous Sun, that you and all your people may enter and still find room to spare. And he hurried home to the Moon, his gentle wife, and together they raised an enormous compound, wide as a field, and sent word to the Water that all was ready.

The Water came. First he sent a ripple to ask, Is it safe to enter? Come, come, called the Sun. So the Water flowed in, and with him came the fish and the crabs and the shining creatures of the deep. Soon the water rose to the knees of the Sun and the Moon. Is there still room? asked the Water. Come, come, said the Sun, though his voice trembled a little.

The Water rose to their waists, and to their shoulders, and still more of his people poured through the gate. The Sun and the Moon climbed onto the roof of their own house, and even there the water followed, lapping at their feet.

At last there was nowhere left to climb but up, up into the wide and empty sky. So the Sun and the Moon rose from the drowned earth and took their places in the heavens, and there they have remained ever since.

And that is why, to this day, the Sun rules the sky by day and the Moon by night, gazing down together upon the Water that once came to visit and never quite left. Remember, my children, a promise made in pride can carry you further from home than you ever meant to go.
