In May 1945, deep inside an Austrian mountain, American soldiers walked into a salt mine and found the greatest hoard of stolen art in human history.

Thousands of paintings. Sculptures. Altarpieces ripped out of cathedrals. Crates stacked in cold tunnels a thousand feet underground, sorted, catalogued, waiting for a museum that would never be built, for a Reich that had just stopped existing.

And in that mountain, in a crate of his own, they found the private treasure of the second most powerful man in Nazi Germany. Hermann Göring. The Reichsmarschall. A man who had looted an entire continent to fill his palace with beauty.

Among his prizes was a painting he had wanted more than almost anything he owned. A biblical scene. Christ and a woman accused of adultery. Signed with two small letters that made art historians go quiet with reverence.

I. V. Meer.

Johannes Vermeer. The Sphinx of Delft. A man who died poor in 1675 and left behind only a handful of canvases, each one worth a fortune, each one a national treasure of the Netherlands.

Göring had paid for it with a fortune in looted masterpieces. And the Dutch government wanted to know one thing above all else.

Who sold a Dutch national treasure to a Nazi?

The answer came back with a name. A failed artist. A morphine addict. A man the critics had laughed out of the art world twenty years earlier.

Two weeks later, that man sat in an Amsterdam jail cell facing a charge that could end with a firing squad. And he looked at his interrogators and said the sentence that would make him the strangest folk hero of the twentieth century.
"I did not sell a national treasure to Hermann Göring."

"I sold him a fake. I painted it myself. And you cannot prove otherwise, because I am the only man alive who can."

This is the story of Han van Meegeren. The man who fooled the greatest experts in the world, who took millions from the Nazi high command, who traded a worthless canvas for a pile of stolen masterpieces, and who then had to commit the hardest act of his life.

He had to convince a courtroom that he was a liar.
Henricus Antonius van Meegeren was born in 1889 in Deventer, a quiet town in the east of the Netherlands. Everyone called him Han.

His father was a schoolteacher. A hard man. A man with a very particular idea of what a son should become, and art was nowhere on the list. The story that followed Han for the rest of his life was that his father would catch him drawing, tear the paper apart, and make the boy repeat a sentence out loud, over and over, like a punishment prayer.

I know nothing. I am nothing. I am capable of nothing.

Think about that for a second. Because everything that comes later, every forgery, every lie, every stolen fortune, every night he spent laughing at the most respected men in Europe, grows out of that sentence like a weed out of a crack.

A boy who was told he was nothing spent the rest of his life demanding that the world admit he was something.

At school he found a teacher who saved him. A man named Bartus Korteling, a painter obsessed with the Dutch Golden Age. Korteling worshipped Vermeer. He did not just admire the paintings. He wanted to know how they were made. He ground his own pigments, the way the old masters had. He talked about lapis lazuli, the blue stone shipped from the mountains of Afghanistan, crushed into a blue so deep that painters called it more precious than gold.

He taught the boy that a painting is not only an image. It is a chemistry. It is a recipe. It is a physical object made of specific things, in a specific order, by specific hands.

Han van Meegeren never forgot that lesson. Decades later, it would make him rich.

He studied architecture in Delft, because his father insisted, and he was good at it, and he hated it. He drifted into painting anyway. In 1913 he won a gold medal for a drawing of a church interior, and for a moment it looked like the world might open for him.

It did not.

He married young. He had children. He painted portraits of wealthy women and society ladies, deer and Christ figures and pretty, sentimental things that sold well enough to keep the lights on. He had a first exhibition in 1917 in The Hague that actually did quite nicely.

And then the critics arrived.
Here is the thing about the art world of the 1920s. It was moving. Fast. Picasso was tearing faces into geometry. The moderns were burning the past. And here was Han van Meegeren, painting like a man born three hundred years too late, technically skilled, emotionally old fashioned, working in a style that was already a museum piece.

The critics did not merely dislike him. They dismissed him. That is worse. Dislike means you matter. Dismissal means you do not.

They called his work derivative. Unoriginal. A polished imitation of the old masters with nothing of his own inside it. One review said, in effect, that he had every gift a painter could want except the only one that counts.

And in that world of critics, one man stood on top of the mountain.

Abraham Bredius.

Bredius was the authority. Not an authority. The authority. Former director of the Mauritshuis in The Hague. The living oracle on Rembrandt and on Vermeer. If Bredius said a painting was genuine, it was genuine. Museums bought on his word. Fortunes moved on his signature. He was in his eighties, living in Monaco, and his verdict was closer to scripture than opinion.

At some point in the 1920s, Van Meegeren was involved in showing Bredius a painting, hoping for a blessing. Bredius took one look and rejected it. Cold. Fast. Final.

You can imagine the boy hearing his father's voice again.

I know nothing. I am nothing. I am capable of nothing.

And somewhere in that humiliation, an idea began to grow. It was not a plan yet. It was closer to a fantasy. A revenge daydream.

What if I painted something so perfect, so exactly like a Vermeer, that Bredius himself declared it a masterpiece?

What if the greatest expert alive stood in front of a Van Meegeren, and wept, and called it holy?

And what if I then stood up in front of the world and said: it is mine. I painted it. You praised my hand and called it Vermeer's, and now everything you have ever said about me is worthless.

That was the dream. The revenge of a mediocre man against the people who told him he was mediocre.

The problem was that the dream required him to become, in secret, one of the most skilled criminals of the twentieth century.
So that is exactly what he did.

To understand what Van Meegeren pulled off, you need to understand why almost nobody can do it.

Faking a Vermeer is not a matter of copying a picture. Any competent art student can copy a picture. The problem is that a painting is a physical object that has been sitting in the world for three hundred years, and time leaves fingerprints all over it.

Here are the walls he had to climb.

Wall number one. The canvas. A brand new canvas is obvious. The weave, the fibers, the tacking edges, all wrong. He needed a genuine seventeenth century canvas.

So he bought cheap, worthless paintings from the correct period. Minor works nobody cared about. Then he scraped them down with a pumice stone, patiently, for days, removing the image while trying to preserve the original ground layer underneath, because that ground layer was the skin of the seventeenth century and he could not fake it.

Wall number two. The pigments. Modern paints contain modern chemicals. A single trace of a pigment invented in the nineteenth century and the whole thing collapses. So he did what his old teacher Korteling taught him. He made his colors himself, from raw materials, the way it was done in the 1600s. He ground lapis lazuli for his blue. Real lapis, imported and expensive, because Vermeer used the real thing and any chemist could tell the difference. He used white lead. He used earths and ochres. He bought badger hair brushes of the correct type, and when he could, he bought old brushes, because a modern bristle left behind in a brushstroke is a confession.

Wall number three, and this is the one that stops everybody.

Time.

Oil paint takes decades to fully harden. Roughly fifty years for it to become truly, deeply solid. There is a simple, brutal test any restorer can perform. You take a cotton swab, dip it in alcohol, and touch it to the surface. On a fresh oil painting, the paint softens. Color lifts. The swab comes away stained.

On a three hundred year old painting, nothing happens. The surface is stone.

Van Meegeren could not wait fifty years. He was in his forties, angry, and out of patience.

So he did something that no forger before him had done properly. He went into the chemistry.

He moved to the south of France in the early 1930s, to a villa on the coast near the Italian border, quiet, private, funded by his portrait work and by a wealthy circle of clients. And for something like four years, in a locked studio, he ran experiments like a man in a laboratory.

He worked out that if he mixed his pigments with a synthetic resin, phenol formaldehyde, the material the world knew as Bakelite, the paint would behave in the most useful way imaginable. When he put the canvas in an oven and baked it at around one hundred degrees, the resin would harden the paint layer into something rigid and insoluble.

Alcohol swab. Nothing lifts. It passes the test.

Now the surface was hard. But it was also smooth, and a genuine old painting is not smooth. It is covered in a fine web of cracks called craquelure, the network of tiny fractures that spreads through a paint film as it ages and the canvas shifts and breathes across centuries.

So he took the baked canvas, and he rolled it. Around a cylinder. Carefully. Cracking the hardened paint film on purpose, in the direction and pattern that age produces.

Then came the detail that separates a good forger from a great one.

Cracks in an old painting are not clean. They are full of three centuries of dust and grime, so the lines read dark under a magnifying glass. So Van Meegeren took India ink, washed it across the surface, worked it down into the fresh cracks, then wiped the top clean. The ink stayed in the fissures. Under a lens, the painting had a lifetime of accumulated dirt sitting exactly where dirt should sit.

Then a coat of varnish, tinted brown, to give it the warm, dim, tobacco colored glow that centuries of candle smoke and old varnish leave behind.
Now think about what that means.

The canvas is period. The ground layer is period. The pigments are period. The paint does not dissolve. The cracks are convincing. The dirt is in the cracks. The varnish is aged.

Every single test that the science of the 1930s could perform on a painting, this object would pass.

There was only one test left. The eye of the expert.

And the eye of the expert, as Van Meegeren understood better than any man alive, is not a scientific instrument.

It is a human being. And a human being can be seduced.
Here is where Van Meegeren does the thing that makes him genuinely, disturbingly brilliant. Not as a painter. As a psychologist.

He did not copy an existing Vermeer. That would be pointless. Every Vermeer was known and catalogued.

Instead he went and studied the scholars. He read what the experts wanted. And he found a hole in the map.

There was a theory circulating in art history circles, championed by Bredius himself, that in his early years Vermeer must have travelled to Italy and fallen under the influence of Caravaggio. That somewhere out there, undiscovered, was a lost group of large religious paintings by the young Vermeer, biblical scenes, in an Italian manner, before he settled into the quiet domestic interiors that made him immortal.

Nobody had ever found one.

It was the great missing chapter. The holy grail of Dutch art history. The painting that would prove the theory and crown the career of whoever authenticated it.

Van Meegeren did not paint what Vermeer painted.

He painted what Bredius desperately wanted Vermeer to have painted.

He called it "The Supper at Emmaus." Christ, at the moment he breaks bread and reveals himself to the disciples who did not recognize him. A large canvas. Sober. Still. Bathed in that famous soft light. Muted blues and yellows. Deliberately restrained, deliberately a little awkward, because it was supposed to be early work, the work of a young genius still finding his voice.

Then he baked it. Rolled it. Inked it. Varnished it.

And then he did the smartest thing in the whole affair. He stayed away.

Van Meegeren never touched the sale. He worked through a lawyer, and a cover story, one of those stories that art dealers love because it explains everything and can be checked by nobody. The painting, it was said, belonged to a Dutch family living abroad in some difficulty, an old collection, a discreet sale, no publicity please.

And in 1937 the painting was carried to Monaco, to an old man in his eighties.

Abraham Bredius.
The report he wrote is one of the most quoted humiliations in the history of connoisseurship. Bredius looked at that canvas and his heart came out of his chest. He described the shock of standing before a masterpiece by Vermeer that no one had known existed. He praised its purity, its untouched surface, the sense of a great work emerging from the dark. He announced it as the finest Vermeer he had ever seen, and he meant it.

The Burlington Magazine published his verdict. The art world detonated.

The Rembrandt Society raised the money. The Museum Boijmans Van Beuningen in Rotterdam acquired "The Supper at Emmaus" for a sum in the region of five hundred and twenty thousand guilders, an enormous fortune at the time, worth many millions in today's money.

They hung it as the crown jewel. Crowds queued. It became a symbol of Dutch national pride, a treasure recovered for the nation on the eve of a war.

And Han van Meegeren stood in that museum, in the crowd, an anonymous failure in a good coat, watching people weep in front of his own brushwork.

Now this is the moment. This is where he steps forward and says, look at me, I painted that, and your greatest expert kissed it and called it Vermeer.

He does not do it.
Why not?

Some say pride. He wanted the world to acknowledge him, yes, but he also wanted the applause to keep going, and a confession ends the applause.

But mostly, I think, it was simpler than that.

He had just learned that he could print money. Half a million guilders. For a few months of work, some old canvas, and a hot oven.

The revenge could wait.

The money could not.
Between 1938 and 1943, Van Meegeren produced a run of forgeries that turned him into one of the wealthiest men in the Netherlands.

"Interior with Drinkers." "The Head of Christ." "The Last Supper." "Isaac Blessing Jacob." "The Washing of Christ's Feet." Vermeers, and a handful of works passed off as other Dutch masters. Each one certified. Each one sold. Each one absorbed into the national collection or into a private one, with the whole authenticating apparatus of Dutch art history standing behind it, nodding, congratulating itself on the recovery of a lost heritage.

Because here is the trap that the experts had walked into, and it is a trap that catches human beings in every field, every day.

Once you have publicly declared that a painting is a masterpiece, the next one that arrives in the same style is not a suspect. It is a confirmation. It proves you were right the first time. Every new forgery made the previous forgery more credible, and every previous forgery made the new one easier to swallow.

The lie was self reinforcing. Doubt would have required the most respected men in Europe to admit, in public, that they had been played like children. So they did not doubt.

Meanwhile the Netherlands fell.

In May 1940, the Germans invaded, and the country was occupied, and the Dutch began five years of hunger, fear, deportation, and death.

And Han van Meegeren got rich.

He bought properties. Dozens of them, by some accounts. Houses, hotels, a nightclub. He filled his home in Amsterdam with luxury. He drank. He took morphine, and had done for years, and the addiction was eating him. He surrounded himself with a fashionable crowd who did not ask hard questions about where a mediocre portrait painter had suddenly found a fortune, in the middle of a war, in a starving city.

He was not a resistance hero. Understand that clearly, because the myth that came later will try to sell you one.

He was a man making an enormous amount of money in an occupied country while his neighbours were burning furniture for heat.

And then a customer came calling. The biggest one of all.
Hermann Göring was a collector in the way that a fire is a collector.

He had the Luftwaffe. He had the Four Year Plan. He was Hitler's designated successor, and he had an appetite that made men nervous. His country estate, Carinhall, north of Berlin, was being turned into a private museum of European civilization, stocked by force from every country the Wehrmacht overran.

His agents were everywhere in the Netherlands. Two names matter here. Alois Miedl, a German banker operating comfortably inside Dutch high society, who had taken over a Jewish owned Amsterdam gallery. And Walter Hofer, Göring's personal art buyer, the man who prowled Europe looking for things his master could not live without.

Göring wanted a Vermeer.

Not just any old master. A Vermeer. It was the ultimate prize. There were only a few dozen in the world. Hitler had been trying to get one for his own planned museum at Linz. Owning one would be a triumph over his rival, over the Dutch, over history itself.

And then, in 1942, word reached Göring's people that a previously unknown Vermeer, a religious scene, was quietly available. From a private Dutch source. Discreetly.

"Christ and the Adulteress."

The negotiations ran through intermediaries. Van Meegeren, of course, kept his hands clean, working at several removes, and the price agreed was extraordinary. Around one and a half million guilders. But it is the way it was paid that makes this story what it is.

Göring did not send cash from Berlin.

He paid in art.

He handed over a large group of paintings, well over a hundred of them, drawn from the pool of works he and his machine had taken from Dutch collections. Real paintings. Genuine works, with genuine owners, many of them Jewish families who had been robbed, deported, murdered.
Now sit with the arithmetic.

Hermann Göring, one of the great looters in the history of the world, handed over a mountain of authentic art in exchange for a single canvas.

That canvas was a piece of Bakelite hardened, oven baked fraud, painted a few months earlier by a morphine addict in Amsterdam, worth precisely nothing.

The Reichsmarschall of Nazi Germany had been swindled out of a fortune, and he hung the swindle in his private gallery, and he stood in front of it, and he was moved.

He kept that painting through the collapse of everything. As the Reich came apart, as the Red Army closed in, as Carinhall was packed up and eventually dynamited on his own orders, the treasure went south to Bavaria and Austria, to the salt mines, where the cold, dry air was perfect for preserving old canvases.

Which brings us back to that mountain.
The mine at Altaussee sat in the Austrian Alps, a labyrinth of tunnels driven into rock.

Inside it, the Nazis had hidden the loot of a continent, thousands upon thousands of objects, and there were orders in the final days, contested and terrifying, that suggested the whole collection might be destroyed rather than surrendered. The mine was saved. Local miners, and Allied officers of the Monuments, Fine Arts and Archives programme, the men history now calls the Monuments Men, walked into that mountain in the summer of 1945 and began the impossible job of working out what everything was and who it had been stolen from.

And they found Göring's personal collection, with his own inventory, meticulously kept, because bureaucratic Nazis wrote everything down.

There, listed as one of his prizes, was a Vermeer. "Christ and the Adulteress."

The Dutch reaction was immediate and furious. That painting had never been in any Dutch catalogue. It had never been in any museum. It had emerged from nowhere and gone straight into the hands of the enemy. So somebody in the Netherlands had found a national treasure and personally sold it to Hermann Göring.

That was not merely bad taste. In 1945, in a country boiling with rage against collaborators, that was treason. Plundering the cultural property of the Netherlands and trafficking with the enemy. The kind of charge that ended in front of a wall.

Allied investigators followed the paper trail back through Miedl, back through the intermediaries, back through the lawyer, back and back, until it stopped in a grand house on the Keizersgracht in Amsterdam.

On the twenty ninth of May 1945, they came for Han van Meegeren.
He held out for weeks. Sitting in a cell. Saying nothing useful. The morphine gone, the withdrawal beginning, the war crimes machinery grinding towards him.

He had one defence. And it was the most humiliating sentence a proud man could possibly speak.

On the twelfth of July 1945, he broke.

The words he is remembered for are close to these. Fools. You think I sold a priceless Vermeer to that man? There was no Vermeer. I painted it myself.

And not just that one, he said. The Supper at Emmaus. The great one. The masterpiece in Rotterdam that the nation had wept over. The one Bredius crowned. Mine. All mine.
Now imagine you are the interrogator.

A collaborator facing a capital charge has just told you that instead of being a traitor, he is the greatest forger in history, and that by the way the pride of the national museum, authenticated by the most respected expert in Europe, purchased with public money, celebrated in newspapers, is a fake he cooked in his oven.

Of course they did not believe him. It was too convenient. It was too insane. It was a drowning man clutching at the most extravagant lie he could invent.

The art establishment did not believe him either, and they had every reason not to. If Van Meegeren was telling the truth, then the entire authority of Dutch connoisseurship was a joke, an old man in Monaco had been catfished by a hack, and a museum had spent a fortune on garbage.

So they set him a test. It was almost a dare.

Prove it.

Paint another one.

And so, in the autumn of 1945, in a house in Amsterdam, under guard, watched by police officers, court appointed witnesses, journalists and experts who came and went, Han van Meegeren set up a canvas and began to paint a new Vermeer.

They gave him his materials. He asked for them by name. He was allowed his alcohol, allegedly, and by some accounts his morphine, because the doctors judged that a man in full withdrawal could not hold a brush steady, and the state needed those hands to work.

He called it "Jesus Among the Doctors." The young Christ in the temple, surrounded by old men who cannot believe what this boy is telling them.

I do not think that subject was an accident.

For weeks he worked, in front of an audience, and the audience watched a Vermeer come out of his hands.

It is one of the strangest performances of the twentieth century. A man painting for his life. Every stroke was a piece of evidence. He was not trying to make a masterpiece. He was trying to prove he was a criminal.
The painting alone was not enough. Skill proves you could have. It does not prove you did.

So the Dutch government convened a commission of scientists and art historians, led by a formidable Belgian conservator named Paul Coremans, and turned the full weight of forensic science onto the disputed Vermeers.

And now, everything Van Meegeren had done so cleverly turned into a signed confession.

They took paint samples. They ran chemical tests. And down in the paint film of "The Supper at Emmaus," the great masterpiece, the pride of Rotterdam, they found phenol formaldehyde.

Bakelite.

A synthetic plastic resin patented in the twentieth century, sitting inside a canvas supposedly painted in the 1600s. Johannes Vermeer did not have access to Bakelite. Nobody in the seventeenth century did.

They found cobalt blue, a nineteenth century pigment, in trace amounts contaminating his precious ultramarine.

They studied the cracks under magnification and saw the truth of them. In a genuinely aged painting the craquelure runs down through the layers, through the varnish and the paint and into the ground, all the way to the canvas, because time cracks the whole object. In these paintings the cracks ran through the upper paint and stopped. And the black in the cracks was not three centuries of dust and grime. It was ink. Sitting on top, washed in, artificial.

They took an X ray. Underneath, they found the ghost of the old, worthless seventeenth century painting he had scraped away, and traces of his abrasion, and they could see how the composition he had painted sat on top of a canvas that had had a different life.

The science was final. It agreed with the confession.

The Vermeers were not Vermeers.

The Netherlands had to swallow the fact that its most celebrated national art discovery of the century had been made in the kitchen oven of a man the critics had told to give up.
The trial opened in Amsterdam in October 1947, and it was a circus in the best sense. The courtroom was packed. Photographers everywhere. The forged paintings themselves were carried in and set on easels in front of the judges, the defendants and the evidence at the same time.

The charge was no longer collaboration. It was forgery and fraud.

And the crowd, the ordinary Dutch public, had made up its mind about Han van Meegeren, and the verdict was extraordinary.

They loved him.

Understand the moment. This was a nation flattened by occupation, humiliated, starved through the terrible winter of 1944 into 1945, robbed of its art, its food, its people. And here was a little Dutchman with a cigarette and a smirk who had taken the fat Reichsmarschall of the Third Reich, the man who bombed Rotterdam flat, the man who looted their country, and sold him a worthless painting for a mountain of stolen treasure. Treasure which came back. Because those hundred and thirty odd paintings Göring paid with were recovered and returned to the Netherlands.

He had swindled Göring, and the swindle had brought Dutch art home.

In the public imagination he stopped being a criminal and became a trickster hero, the clever little man who cheated the giant. Polls of the period suggested he was one of the most popular men in the country.

The court was less romantic. It convicted him of forgery and fraud on the twelfth of November 1947 and sentenced him to one year in prison. The minimum. Not a day more.

He never served it.

His heart, wrecked by years of alcohol and morphine and the strain of the most exhausting performance any artist has ever given, gave out. He suffered a heart attack, then another. On the thirtieth of December 1947, six weeks after his conviction, Han van Meegeren died in a clinic in Amsterdam. He was fifty eight years old.

He died famous. Which is, in the end, the only thing he ever actually wanted.
There is one more scene, and it is my favourite in the entire story.

Hermann Göring was in custody at Nuremberg, on trial for his life, when the news reached him.

The Vermeer. The prize. The one thing he had wanted more than almost anything, the painting he had traded a hundred and thirty seven stolen masterpieces to possess, the object he had stared at and been moved by.

It was a fake. Painted a few years earlier by a Dutch morphine addict who had used him.

There is an account from a Dutch officer present, and the line has been repeated ever since because it is too good to lose. When Göring was told, he looked as if for the first time he had discovered that there was evil in the world.
Let that sit.

A man who had helped build a machine that murdered millions. A man who had stripped a continent of its art and its wealth and its life. And what shocked him, what finally introduced Hermann Göring to the concept of human wickedness, was the discovery that somebody had cheated him in a business deal.

He died by his own hand within months.

Van Meegeren outlived him. Barely.
Now here is where I have to spoil the folk tale a little, because the folk tale is not the whole truth, and you deserve the whole truth.

Han van Meegeren was not a patriot. He was not a resistance fighter. He did not set out to cheat the Nazis for the honour of the Netherlands.

He was a man who wanted money and revenge, and the Nazis happened to be the richest customers in Europe.

He sold to them. He prospered under the occupation while his countrymen starved. He moved in circles that were comfortable with the new order. And after the war, a book of his drawings turned up in the ruins of the Reich Chancellery library, in Hitler's own collection, carrying an inscription. The exact wording and the exact circumstances have been argued over for decades, and Van Meegeren always denied writing it. But it does not look like the library of a resistance hero.

He was, in the end, a wounded, vain, self pitying man with an extraordinary gift and a rotten character, who fooled experts he despised and then discovered that the whole world wanted to call it heroism.

And yet.

And yet the fact remains, cold and hard and beautiful.

He took a fortune off Hermann Göring. He fed the Third Reich a lie and got a hundred and thirty seven genuine paintings in return, and every one of them came home to the country the Nazis had robbed. He humiliated the greatest experts of his age so completely that art authentication changed forever, and the sciences of pigment analysis and forensic examination came in, and today no museum on earth would trust a single old man's eye the way they trusted Bredius.

And there is one final, quiet, delicious thing that almost nobody thinks about.

Go and look at "The Supper at Emmaus" today, and it looks nothing like a Vermeer. Not really. The proportions are strange. The faces are heavy. The mood is off. Once you know, you cannot unsee it. Experts today wonder how anyone was ever fooled.

But they were fooled. All of them. The best in the world.

Because Van Meegeren's real genius was never in the brush.

It was in understanding that people do not see what is in front of them.

They see what they are hoping to find.
He gave them their dream, and they paid him millions for it, and one of them was a Reichsmarschall.

I am not going to call him a hero.

But I will admit this. Of all the men who ever picked the pocket of the Third Reich, none of them did it with a paintbrush.
