Long ago, in the days when the animals of the forest still spoke as people do, Tortoise was known far and wide as the cleverest and the greediest of them all. He was never cruel, only hungry, and his hunger made him cunning.

One year a terrible famine came upon the land. The rains forgot to fall, the yams shrank in the earth, and every belly in the village grumbled through the long dry days. But high above, in the sky country, the birds still feasted. For the sky people had invited all the birds to a great banquet among the clouds.

When Tortoise heard of the feast, his mouth watered. He went from bird to bird, begging each for a single feather, until he had stitched himself a fine cloak of many colours. Dressed in borrowed plumage, he flapped his arms and rose with the flock into the sky.

Before they arrived, sly Tortoise spoke to the birds. In the sky country, he said, it is the custom for every guest to take a new name for the feast. And I, said Tortoise, will be called All of You. The birds thought this a harmless game and agreed.

When the sky people spread the food upon their mats, they announced, This feast is prepared for all of you. Tortoise leapt forward. Did you hear? he cried. The food is for me, for I am named All of You. And he ate. He ate the pounded yam and the rich palm-oil stew, he ate until his shell was tight, while the hungry birds watched in silence.

But the birds do not forget an insult. One by one, quietly, each plucked back the feather she had lent him, until Tortoise stood bare upon the edge of the sky with no way to fly home.

Call my wife, he begged a passing parrot. Tell her to bring out all the soft things in our house, and pile them beneath our compound, so I may jump down and land gently. But the parrot, still angry, carried a different message. It told the wife to bring out all the hard things, the hoes and the spears and the stones.

So Tortoise let go of the sky and fell, faster and faster, down toward his waiting home. He landed not on softness but upon the hard heap, and his smooth shell shattered into a hundred pieces.

His wife gathered the broken bits and a healer glued them back together, but the seams never smoothed. And that, my children, is why to this day the shell of the tortoise is not sleek and shining, but cracked and stitched with many small lines, a map of the day greed reached too far into the sky.
